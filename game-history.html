Introduction

Valorant, developed and published by Riot Games, is a free-to-play tactical hero shooter released on June 2, 2020, for Windows. The game began as Project A and entered a closed beta phase on April 7, 2020. Drawing inspiration from Counter-Strike, Valorant focuses on team-based tactical gameplay.

Gameplay

Valorant is a team-based tactical shooter where players select Agents, each with unique abilities, and compete in attacking or defending teams. Abilities require charges, and the economic system allows purchasing weapons and ability charges based on game outcomes.

Game Modes

Valorant offers various game modes including Unrated, Spike Rush, Swiftplay, Competitive, Deathmatch, Team Deathmatch, Escalation, Replication, and Snowball Fight. Each mode offers different objectives, round structures, and gameplay elements.

Agents

Agents in Valorant are divided into four roles: Duelists, Sentinels, Initiators, and Controllers. Each role has specific characteristics and abilities tailored for different playstyles, tactics, and strategies.

Development

Valorant was developed by Riot Games starting in 2014. The team aimed to make tactical shooters and esports accessible to a broad audience. The game was built using Unreal Engine 4 with a focus on gameplay and optimization.

Reception

Valorant received positive reviews from critics and players alike, achieving high scores from various gaming publications. It gained massive viewership on Twitch during its beta phase and has garnered a substantial player base since its release.

Controversies

Valorant faced criticism for issues related to its toxic voice communication system and highly competitive environment. The game's anti-cheat software, Vanguard, was also a subject of controversy due to concerns regarding privacy and system access.

Mobile Spin-off

Riot Games announced plans for a mobile version of Valorant in June 2021, aiming to expand the game's universe and reach a wider audience through mobile platforms.

Windows 11 Compatibility

Valorant faced compatibility issues with Windows 11 due to specific hardware requirements, such as the Trusted Platform Module (TPM) 2.0 compliance and UEFI secure boot, mandated by Microsoft.
